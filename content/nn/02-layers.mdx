One neuron can only learn one simple pattern. Stack many of them side-by-side — each reading the **same inputs** but computing different things — and you get a **layer**.

Play with the layer directly. Start at 1 neuron, then add more:

<LayerExplorer />

**Watch the graph.** At 1 neuron, three inputs funnel into a single squashed number — the whole network is a narrow bottleneck. At 4 or 8 neurons, the signal has room to branch: one neuron might end up caring mostly about size, another about location, another about a combination.

This is the whole reason layers exist: to give the network **parallel paths through the data**, so different neurons can specialize.

## The math of a layer

If a layer has $m$ neurons receiving $n$ inputs, it's just lesson 1's math stacked into a matrix:

$$
\mathbf{z} = W \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}, \qquad \mathbf{a} = f(\mathbf{z})
$$

- $W$ is an $m \times n$ matrix — one **row** per neuron (that neuron's weights)
- $\mathbf{b}$ is a length-$m$ bias vector
- $f$ is the activation function applied elementwise

Every single number in $W$ is what you just dragged in lesson 1. A layer is just: more of the same, in parallel.

## Why more neurons (usually) help

More neurons → more capacity → the network can represent a more complex function. That's the good part.

But capacity is a double-edged sword: too many neurons on too little data, and the network memorizes specific examples instead of learning the underlying pattern. That failure mode is called **overfitting**, and lesson 10 puts it on the screen.
