Press **Step** a few times and pause on the **Prediction** phase. The phase trail tells you two numbers: the model's output, and the actual label. The gap between them is the error for this single training example.

## Error, formally

For a single example:

$$
e = \hat{y} - y
$$

where $\hat{y}$ is the prediction and $y$ is the truth. Sign matters — a positive error means the model overshot; negative means it undershot. For regression with a single output neuron, $e$ is a scalar. For multi-output tasks it's a vector.

## Why we don't just add up errors

You might think: *"average the errors over the training set — that's the thing we want to minimize."* It almost works, except:

- Positive and negative errors cancel. A model that's off by +10 half the time and −10 the other half would average to zero error. It's terrible, but its average-error says it's perfect.

That's why every useful loss function — the thing we'll actually minimize — makes errors **positive-definite** first. The next lesson introduces the most common one: **mean squared error**.

## "Prediction vs. reality" vs. "loss"

Quick vocabulary check so things don't collide later:

- **Prediction** — what the model says: $\hat{y} = a^{(L)}$
- **Reality** (target, label, ground truth) — what the example is labeled with: $y$
- **Error** — the signed gap: $e = \hat{y} - y$
- **Loss** — a non-negative, increasing-with-badness summary of errors across a whole batch. Introduced next lesson.
