A **loss function** is a single number that says: *how bad was the model on this training example?* Training is "change the weights to make this number smaller." That's the whole game.

## Mean Squared Error (MSE)

For regression with a scalar target, the workhorse is **MSE**:

$$
L(\hat{y}, y) = (\hat{y} - y)^2
$$

For a whole dataset of $N$ samples:

$$
\bar{L} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \bigl(\hat{y}_i - y_i\bigr)^2
$$

## Why squared?

Three reasons:

1. **It's positive.** No more cancellation between over- and under-predictions.
2. **It punishes big errors more.** An error of 2 costs 4; an error of 10 costs 100. The model learns to prioritize *not being wildly wrong* over being a little off.
3. **Its derivative is clean.** $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = 2(\hat{y} - y)$. Simple chain rule from there; no absolute values, no kinks. This matters a lot for the next lesson.

## Loss is what gets minimized

Everything after this lesson is about **turning the loss number into a pressure on each weight** to change. The whole training loop is:

1. Forward pass → produce $\hat{y}$
2. Loss → compute $L(\hat{y}, y)$
3. Backward pass → figure out *how to change each weight to make $L$ smaller*
4. Update → actually change them

You already have (1) and (2). Next: **backpropagation**, the clever bit that makes (3) possible.
