A neuron takes a few numbers in, does a tiny bit of math, and sends one number out. That's the whole building block. Play with one:

<NeuronPlayground />

The three inputs are **features of a house** — how big it is, how many bedrooms, how good the location. The neuron combines them into a single predicted **price**.

The **weights** decide *how much each feature matters*. Drag a weight to **0** and that feature stops affecting the price entirely. Drag it **negative** (red edge) and that feature now pulls the price *down*. The blue/red line-thickness you see on each edge is literally what the weight is doing, made visible.

Try the three preset houses at the top. A mansion with the same weights predicts a much higher price than a small apartment — because its inputs are bigger, and the neuron's math amplifies them through the weights.

## The math, in one line

$$
y = f\!\left(\sum_{i} w_i\, x_i \;+\; b\right)
$$

- $x_i$ — an **input** (a feature of the house — you just dragged these)
- $w_i$ — a **weight** (how much that feature matters — you dragged these too)
- $b$ — a **bias** (a constant the neuron always adds)
- $f$ — an **activation function** (the switch at the top of the playground — try each one and see how the output changes shape)

Every neural network you'll ever see is built from this one formula, stacked and repeated. Next lesson: stack many of these into a **layer**.
