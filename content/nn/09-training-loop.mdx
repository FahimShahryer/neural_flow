Every piece is now in place. The **training loop** is what happens when you put them in order and hit Play:

```
for each epoch:
  for each training sample:
    (1) forward pass     → get prediction
    (2) loss             → how wrong were we
    (3) backprop         → gradients for every weight
    (4) update           → w ← w − η · ∂L/∂w
```

That's neural network training. The whole thing. Every one of those steps is one phase in the sim below.

## One epoch at a time

An **epoch** is one full pass over the training set. With 200 samples and 7 phases per sample, that's 1,400 steps per epoch. The loss chart records the average training loss per epoch — one point per pass.

Watch what happens over time:

- **Early epochs** — loss drops fast. Big gradients, easy wins. The network goes from random guesses to something vaguely reasonable in a few passes.
- **Middle epochs** — loss drops slower. The network has the broad pattern; it's refining details.
- **Late epochs** — loss flattens. The network has extracted about as much signal as it can. Further training mostly reshuffles noise.

You'll see all three regimes in the live chart below if you let it run 30–50 epochs.

## When to stop

Three common stopping rules in production:

1. **Fixed epoch count** — set `epochs = 100` and done. Simple, often fine.
2. **Loss threshold** — stop when loss drops below some value. Problem: you often don't know what's achievable.
3. **Early stopping on validation loss** — train until a *held-out* set's loss stops improving. The gold standard. Requires a validation set, which our engine doesn't model (yet).

Our sim runs until you press Pause. Useful for watching; in production you'd always have a stopping rule.

## What's actually changing

Click a weight in the Inspector right before hitting Play. Keep that weight selected. The sparkline under the Inspector shows the history of **that exact number** as training progresses. That's not a mock. That's the literal SGD trajectory of that one parameter, drawn in real time.

Every success story you've heard in AI — image recognition, language models, protein folding — is, under the hood, that same sparkline repeated across billions of weights.

## One last lesson

The loss chart going down is great. But it can lie: a model that's "trained perfectly" on a small training set can still be useless on new data. That's called **overfitting**, and it's what the final lesson of this module unpacks.
