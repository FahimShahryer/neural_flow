Backprop produced a gradient for every weight. **Gradient descent** is the rule that turns those gradients into weight updates:

$$
w \;\leftarrow\; w \;-\; \eta \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial w}
$$

That's it. Subtract a little bit of the gradient (scaled by the **learning rate** $\eta$) from the weight. Do it for every weight and every bias. That's one SGD step.

Press Step until you hit the **Weight update** phase. Click any weight. The Inspector shows a live formula:

> next ≈ `w` − `lr` · `∂L/∂w`

That's the update being computed *for the weight you just clicked*, visible before it actually happens. Every SGD step is this formula applied to every single parameter.

## Why the minus sign

Gradients point in the direction of **steepest increase** of the loss. We want loss to **decrease**. So we step in the opposite direction: negative of the gradient. A positive gradient → decrease the weight. Negative gradient → increase it.

## The learning rate is a whole story

Three tunable knobs in neural-network training matter more than learning rate put together. The learning rate is *that* important.

Go to the config panel on the right of the demo above. Drag the **Learning rate** slider. Then press Play.

- **Too high** (0.5+) → each update overshoots the minimum. Loss bounces wildly, possibly diverges. Load the **Explosion** preset above to see the extreme.
- **Too low** (0.001) → updates are so small the loss crawls for hundreds of epochs. Load the **Crawl** preset to see the extreme.
- **Just right** (0.01 – 0.1 for this problem) → loss descends steadily.

There is no universal "right" learning rate — it depends on the network, the data, the loss surface. In practice people use **learning rate schedules** (start high, decrease over time) and adaptive optimizers like Adam that automate a lot of this. For now: appreciate that one hyperparameter shapes whether training converges at all.

## Stochastic, mini-batch, and full batch

What we've been doing is **stochastic** gradient descent — updating weights after every single training sample. Two cousins:

- **Mini-batch** — average the gradients over a small batch (32, 64, 128 samples) before updating. Smoother, GPU-friendly. The default in practice.
- **Full batch** — gradient of the entire training set before one update. Mathematically purest but slow; no regularizing noise from sampling.

Our engine runs stochastic — each sample gets its own update. Watch the loss chart: it's noisy within an epoch but trends down across epochs. That noise is actually *useful* — it helps SGD escape bad local minima in deeper networks.

With updates happening every sample, the next step is obvious: **run this over and over** until the loss stops dropping. That's the **training loop**.
