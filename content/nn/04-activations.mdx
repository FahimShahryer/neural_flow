An **activation function** sits at the end of every neuron. It takes the weighted sum $z = \sum_i w_i x_i + b$ and decides what the neuron actually outputs.

Without one, every layer collapses into a plain linear combination — ten layers or one, it's the same. Activations are what give networks their *non-linearity* — their ability to bend and curve to fit complex patterns.

## The canonical four

**Linear** — does nothing. $f(z) = z$. Useful as the *output* activation for regression (you want an unrestricted number, not a squashed one). Never use it on hidden layers: stacking linears is just one big linear.

**ReLU** — $f(z) = \max(0, z)$. Cheapest non-linearity there is. Passes positives straight through, zeroes out negatives. The default hidden activation in almost every modern network, because it's fast and doesn't saturate.

**Sigmoid** — $f(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$. Squashes any real number into $(0, 1)$. Natural for "probability of yes." Downside: it *saturates* (goes flat) for large $|z|$, which causes the **vanishing gradient** problem when stacked deep.

**Tanh** — $f(z) = \tanh(z)$. Sigmoid's zero-centered cousin, into $(-1, 1)$. Gradients are a bit healthier than sigmoid, but it saturates too.

ReLU trains fast but can produce "dead" neurons that output 0 forever. Sigmoid on the hidden layer trains more slowly and can vanish. Tanh is usually a fine middle ground on shallow nets.

## The rule of thumb

- Hidden layers → **ReLU** (or its variants) until you have a reason to switch
- Binary output → **Sigmoid**
- Regression output → **Linear**
- Classification output (multi-class) → *softmax*, a cousin of sigmoid we'll meet in a future module
